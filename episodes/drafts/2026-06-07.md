## Working Episode Title

Courting Disappointment

## Hook

Some disappointments surprise us.

Others send a calendar invite first.

They do not just happen to us. We court them. We give them attention. We save them a seat. We keep checking the phone, waiting for something to love us back that has already shown us what it is.

That can happen in relationships. It can happen with ambition. It can happen with approval. It can happen with the picture we built in our head of how life was supposed to go.

## Devotional Body

Proverbs 7:1 begins with a father's voice telling his son to keep his words and store up his commands. Before the chapter gets to the danger of desire being pulled in the wrong direction, it starts with storage.

That matters.

Because temptation rarely introduces itself as destruction. It usually shows up as relief. As attention. As finally. As the thing you think will quiet the ache.

Desire is powerful, but desire is not always honest. It can make promises it does not have the authority to keep. It can tell you, "This will fix it," when all it really means is, "This will distract you for a little while."

That is why wisdom has to be kept close before the room gets loud.

Proverbs does not begin by saying, "Try harder in the dangerous moment." It says, "Keep my words." Put wisdom somewhere deeper than your mood. Store it before the ache starts negotiating.

A father knows this. Not because he is above desire, but because he has had to learn what desire does when it gets lonely. He has seen want turn into leaning, leaning turn into needing, and needing turn into bargaining.

That is how disappointment gets courted. Not always through rebellion. Sometimes through imagination that never gets submitted to wisdom.

Wisdom does not kill desire. Wisdom disciples desire. It teaches desire where to walk, what to wait for, and when to stop knocking on a door that keeps taking peace as payment.

And that is important, because some doors look harmless when you are lonely. Some expectations sound reasonable when you are tired. Some attachments feel spiritual because you have prayed about them, when really you have only asked God to bless what wisdom has already warned you about.

A fatherly word does not come to embarrass desire. It comes to protect it. It says, "Son, daughter, keep these words close because the moment will come when your feelings make a very convincing argument."

That is when stored wisdom becomes mercy.

So today, keep the word close. Not as decoration. Not as religious backup. Keep it close enough to interrupt you.

Close enough to slow your hand before you send the text.

Close enough to quiet the fantasy before it becomes a plan.

Close enough to remind you that not everything you want is wise enough to lead you.

## Remember

Wisdom kept close can keep desire from keeping bad company.
