## Working Episode Title

Courting Disappointment

## Hook

Words can make promises the heart has not prepared to keep.

That is one reason disappointment shows up so often after big talk. Somebody said the right thing. Somebody painted the picture. Somebody created an expectation.

But the fruit never came.

## Devotional Body

Proverbs 13:2 connects speech with consequence. The mouth is not just noise. It produces fruit. What we say feeds something.

That is a serious thought in a culture where words move fast.

We can post before we process. Promise before we count the cost. Flatter before we tell the truth. Speak peace while planting confusion. Say "I got you" when we have not decided whether we are willing to be dependable.

And then disappointment gets courted.

Not because desire is wrong, but because speech created an expectation that character did not support.

That can happen in romance, in leadership, in friendship, in public life, and around the dinner table. We can use words to build a world we are not willing to inhabit. We can describe a future we have not disciplined ourselves to walk toward.

That is when language becomes dangerous. Not because words are bad, but because words are powerful. They can create hope. And when hope is created carelessly, disappointment has somewhere to land.

The wise person learns to respect that power.

This is where the citizen angle matters. Our words shape public trust. They shape family trust too. A home can only absorb so much overpromising before people stop believing the next sentence.

Proverbs is practical about this. It does not treat speech as decoration. It treats speech as seed. Something grows from it. Either nourishment or trouble. Either trust or suspicion. Either peace or a mess somebody else has to clean up.

So the question is not only, "Was it true?"

The question is also, "What did it feed?"

Did my words feed wisdom?

Did they feed resentment?

Did they feed an expectation I have no intention of honoring?

Did they feed a fantasy because I liked being admired in the moment?

Courting disappointment through speech often starts when we enjoy the emotional reward of saying something more than the responsibility of becoming someone trustworthy.

But wisdom slows the mouth down.

It teaches us to speak words that have fruit behind them. Words that do not just sound good, but can be lived. Words that make the people around us safer, not more uncertain.

Today, let your mouth be honest enough to feed trust.

Say less if you need to.

Mean more when you do.

## Remember

Words feed the future before you arrive there.

## Prayer

Lord, make my words trustworthy.
