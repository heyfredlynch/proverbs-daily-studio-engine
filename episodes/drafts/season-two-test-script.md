# Courting Disappointment

Date: 2026-06-07

Scripture: Proverbs 7:1

## Reader Version

### Scripture Citation

Proverbs 7:1

### Scripture Reading

"My son, keep my words, and lay up my commandments with thee." Proverbs 7:1, King James Version.

### Today's Episode

Some disappointments surprise us.

Others send a calendar invite first.

They do not just happen to us. We court them. We give them attention. We save them a seat. We keep checking our phone, waiting for something to love us back that has already shown us what it is.

That can happen in relationships. It can happen with ambition. It can happen with approval. It can even happen with our own expectations. We start wanting a good thing, but somewhere along the way, wanting becomes leaning. Leaning becomes needing. Needing becomes bargaining.

And then disappointment walks in like it had a key.

### Devotional

Proverbs 7 opens with a father's voice:

"My son, keep my words, and lay up my commandments with thee."

Before the chapter warns about seduction, impulse, and desire getting led by the wrong voice, it starts with storage. Keep my words. Lay up my commandments.

That phrase "lay up" carries the feel of treasuring something, storing it where you can reach it when the moment gets loud. Wisdom is not just something you admire on a calm morning. Wisdom is something you keep close enough to interrupt you.

Because temptation rarely announces itself as destruction. It usually shows up as relief. As attention. As finally. As the thing you think will quiet the ache.

A father knows this. Not because he is above desire, but because he has had to learn how desire behaves when it gets lonely. Desire is powerful, but it is not always honest. It can make promises it does not have the authority to keep.

So Proverbs does not begin by saying, "Try harder in the dangerous moment." It says, "Store wisdom before the dangerous moment arrives."

Keep my words.

Lay them up.

Put them somewhere deeper than your mood.

That is how you stop courting disappointment. Not by pretending you do not want anything. Not by becoming cold or cynical. But by letting wisdom sit close enough to ask better questions.

Is this desire leading me toward life, or just toward escape?

Is this expectation rooted in love, or in control?

Am I receiving a gift, or trying to make an idol keep me company?

When the word is stored in you, it can become a holy interruption. A quiet fatherly voice in the middle of a loud emotional room.

And sometimes that voice saves you from opening the door.

### Remember

Wisdom kept close can keep desire from keeping bad company.
