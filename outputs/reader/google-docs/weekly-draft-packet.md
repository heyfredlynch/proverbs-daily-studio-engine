## Working Episode Title

Courting Disappointment

## Hook

Some disappointments surprise us.

Others send a calendar invite first.

They do not just happen to us. We court them. We give them attention. We save them a seat. We keep checking the phone, waiting for something to love us back that has already shown us what it is.

That can happen in relationships. It can happen with ambition. It can happen with approval. It can happen with the picture we built in our head of how life was supposed to go.

## Devotional Body

Proverbs 7:1 begins with a father's voice telling his son to keep his words and store up his commands. Before the chapter gets to the danger of desire being pulled in the wrong direction, it starts with storage.

That matters.

Because temptation rarely introduces itself as destruction. It usually shows up as relief. As attention. As finally. As the thing you think will quiet the ache.

Desire is powerful, but desire is not always honest. It can make promises it does not have the authority to keep. It can tell you, "This will fix it," when all it really means is, "This will distract you for a little while."

That is why wisdom has to be kept close before the room gets loud.

Proverbs does not begin by saying, "Try harder in the dangerous moment." It says, "Keep my words." Put wisdom somewhere deeper than your mood. Store it before the ache starts negotiating.

A father knows this. Not because he is above desire, but because he has had to learn what desire does when it gets lonely. He has seen want turn into leaning, leaning turn into needing, and needing turn into bargaining.

That is how disappointment gets courted. Not always through rebellion. Sometimes through imagination that never gets submitted to wisdom.

Wisdom does not kill desire. Wisdom disciples desire. It teaches desire where to walk, what to wait for, and when to stop knocking on a door that keeps taking peace as payment.

And that is important, because some doors look harmless when you are lonely. Some expectations sound reasonable when you are tired. Some attachments feel spiritual because you have prayed about them, when really you have only asked God to bless what wisdom has already warned you about.

A fatherly word does not come to embarrass desire. It comes to protect it. It says, "Son, daughter, keep these words close because the moment will come when your feelings make a very convincing argument."

That is when stored wisdom becomes mercy.

So today, keep the word close. Not as decoration. Not as religious backup. Keep it close enough to interrupt you.

Close enough to slow your hand before you send the text.

Close enough to quiet the fantasy before it becomes a plan.

Close enough to remind you that not everything you want is wise enough to lead you.

## Remember

Wisdom kept close can keep desire from keeping bad company.

## Prayer

Lord, let wisdom interrupt me first.

## Today's Challenge

Name one expectation you have been feeding. Ask whether it is forming patience, control, trust, or resentment.

- Places for Fred texture: Proverbs Daily development, technology and AI, Baylor systems, chaplaincy inside institutional process.
- Potential illustrations: A dashboard with missing context; an automated email sent at the wrong emotional moment; wisdom calling louder than the system notification.

## Scripture Citation

Proverbs 8:1

## Verse Text

Verse text is not included in the source brief.

## Working Episode Title

Grace In The Machine

## Hook

Machines can process information faster than we can.

But speed is not wisdom.

A system can sort the file, send the reminder, run the workflow, and still miss the human being standing in front of it. The machine may be efficient. The question is whether the room is wise.

## Devotional Body

Proverbs 8:1 gives us wisdom as a voice. Wisdom is not hidden in a private corner, waiting for only the elite to find her. Wisdom calls out.

That image matters in a world full of systems.

We live inside machines now. Not just computers, but processes, platforms, schedules, insurance forms, hospital charts, school portals, church calendars, algorithms, and notifications. They help us. They also shape us.

And somewhere inside all that motion, Proverbs reminds us that wisdom still has a voice.

Wisdom calls.

Not just in a sanctuary. Not just in a quiet morning. Wisdom calls in the middle of decision-making, public life, work, pressure, commerce, and culture. Wisdom calls where people are trying to figure out what to do next.

That means grace does not have to be outside the machine. Grace can show up inside the process when a person pauses long enough to listen.

Grace in the machine is the nurse who sees the patient, not just the chart. It is the manager who remembers the employee is not a metric. It is the parent who realizes the calendar is full but the child still needs eye contact. It is the creator using technology without surrendering discernment to it.

It is also the moment you refuse to let a tool become a throne. The spreadsheet can help you count, but it cannot tell you what matters. The app can remind you what is next, but it cannot tell you who needs tenderness. The model can generate language, but it cannot carry love for the person who will receive it.

That is not anti-technology. That is pro-wisdom.

Proverbs does not ask us to fear the public square. It asks us to bring wisdom into it. Into the workflow. Into the meeting. Into the hospital hallway. Into the prompt box. Into the decision before the decision hardens into policy.

The danger is not that machines exist. The danger is when we let the machine become the loudest voice in the room.

Proverbs 8:1 reminds us that wisdom is still speaking. And wisdom sounds different from urgency. Urgency says, "Respond now." Wisdom asks, "What is right?" Urgency says, "Optimize." Wisdom asks, "Who is being formed by this?"

So today, do not just ask whether the system works.

Ask whether wisdom is being heard inside it.

Because the goal is not to become less human so the machine can run better. The goal is to become more wise, more attentive, more gracious, even when the gears are turning.

## Remember

Efficiency can move the process, but wisdom must lead the person.

## Prayer

Lord, let wisdom be louder than urgency.

## Today's Challenge

Before trusting one system today, pause and ask what human need the system might be missing.

- Places for Fred texture: ACPE/CPE training, fatherhood, mentorship, learning in public, spiritual formation.
- Potential illustrations: A classroom you were nervous to enter; an invitation with your name on it; wisdom setting a table for beginners.

## Scripture Citation

Proverbs 9:4

## Verse Text

Verse text is not included in the source brief.

## Working Episode Title

Wisdom Invites The Unfinished

## Hook

There are rooms we do not enter because we think we should already know enough to belong there.

We tell ourselves, "I am not ready."

Sometimes that is humility. Sometimes it is fear wearing a responsible outfit.

## Devotional Body

Proverbs 9:4 gives us a picture of wisdom inviting the simple, the unfinished, the ones who still need formation. Wisdom is not embarrassed by beginners.

That is good news.

Because most of us are more unfinished than we admit. We know enough to look competent in certain rooms, but there are still places in us that need teaching. Places where we react before we reflect. Places where we defend before we listen. Places where we confuse being experienced with being wise.

Wisdom does not wait until we are polished to call us.

Wisdom invites us while we are still learning.

That means the invitation is not an insult. When wisdom says, "Come here," it is not shaming you for what you do not know. It is opening a door to what you can become.

A father understands this tension. You want your children to grow, but you do not want them to think love is waiting on the other side of mastery. You invite them into learning because you already love them, not because they have already figured everything out.

God's wisdom works that way too.

It calls to the unformed places in us, not to condemn them, but to disciple them.

The proud person hears an invitation and feels attacked. The teachable person hears an invitation and feels hope.

That may be the real test. Not whether you know everything. Not whether you have outgrown correction. But whether you can still receive an invitation to learn without needing to protect your image.

Sometimes the most spiritual thing you can say is, "I do not know yet."

That sentence can feel small, but it opens a large door. It lets a mentor speak. It lets a child ask another question. It lets God meet you somewhere other than your performance. It gives wisdom room to do more than confirm what you already thought.

There is a kind of maturity that looks like certainty. But there is a deeper maturity that can stay curious without falling apart.

A father does not need to have every answer to be faithful. A disciple does not need to master every lesson before entering the room. Wisdom is not calling the finished. Wisdom is calling the willing.

Wisdom has a table. The question is whether we are humble enough to sit down.

Today, do not despise the place where you are still a student. That may be the very place wisdom is calling your name.

## Remember

Wisdom does not shame the unfinished; it invites them.

## Prayer

Lord, keep me teachable today.

## Today's Challenge

Identify one area where you are still learning. Ask one honest question instead of pretending you already know.

- Alternative title ideas: Cheap Talk Gets Expensive; Words Cost More Than You Think; Noise Has Consequences.
- Places for Fred texture: Citizen voice, public discourse, family conflict, music and lyrical responsibility.
- Potential illustrations: A receipt for words; a cheap post with an expensive consequence; hunger for attention versus hunger for righteousness.

## Scripture Citation

Proverbs 10:3

## Verse Text

Verse text is not included in the source brief.

## Working Episode Title

Cheep Speech Ain't Free

## Hook

Some talk feels cheap because it is easy to spend.

A comment. A joke. A post. A little exaggeration. A little heat because the room was boring and somebody wanted a spark.

But cheap speech still sends a bill.

## Devotional Body

Proverbs 10:3 speaks about hunger, desire, and what God does not abandon. It reminds us that appetite is never just physical. We hunger for food, but we also hunger for attention, control, approval, revenge, and the feeling of being right.

And when that hunger gets loud, the mouth often moves first.

That is why speech and desire are connected. We say things because something in us wants to be fed. We want the laugh. We want the win. We want the last word. We want to feel bigger than the person we just made smaller.

But Proverbs keeps dragging us back to wisdom: desire has consequences.

Words are not weightless. They build trust or break it. They heal a room or poison it. They reveal whether our hunger is being governed by wisdom or driven by impulse.

In public life, this matters. In family, it matters even more. The people closest to us often hear the words we would edit before saying anywhere else. We can call it honesty, but sometimes it is just hunger with no discipline.

Sometimes the mouth is not telling the truth. It is auditioning for power.

It wants to win the argument. It wants to control the story. It wants somebody to clap, repost, agree, or back down. And in that moment, the issue is not vocabulary. The issue is appetite.

That is why wisdom has to reach deeper than speech technique. You can learn how to sound calm and still be cruel. You can learn how to sound spiritual and still be manipulative. Proverbs is after the heart that is feeding the sentence.

The Lord is not indifferent to hunger. Proverbs does not tell us to pretend we do not want anything. It shows us that God cares about appetite, provision, righteousness, and the direction desire takes.

So before the mouth spends what the heart cannot afford, wisdom asks a better question.

What am I hungry for right now?

Am I hungry for peace, or for control?

Am I hungry for truth, or for attention?

Am I hungry for justice, or just the pleasure of landing a clean hit?

Cheep speech ain't free. The misspelling almost tells the truth. It chirps fast, makes noise, and flies away. But the people who heard it still have to live with what landed.

Today, let wisdom govern your hunger before your mouth starts spending.

## Remember

Words are where hidden hunger learns to speak.

## Prayer

Lord, govern my hunger and my mouth.

## Today's Challenge

Before one difficult conversation today, name what you are hungry for and choose words that serve wisdom.

## Scripture Citation

Proverbs 11:1

## Verse Text

Verse text is not included in the source brief.

## Working Episode Title

Make The World A Better Place

## Hook

Most of us will not change the whole world before lunch.

But we can change the weight of a room.

We can make the next interaction more honest, more generous, more human. And sometimes that is where justice starts.

## Devotional Body

Proverbs 11:1 brings wisdom into the marketplace. It speaks about honest measures, about the way people weigh value and treat one another when money, power, and advantage are in the room.

That is not abstract spirituality. That is everyday righteousness.

Wisdom cares about scales because wisdom cares about people. A dishonest scale does not just mismeasure grain. It mismeasures a neighbor. It says, "I can take a little more from you because I can get away with it."

That spirit still exists.

It shows up when we shade the truth to protect our advantage. When we give someone less attention because they cannot help us. When we make the room better for important people and colder for everybody else.

And the uncomfortable part is that most injustice does not begin with someone announcing, "I would like to be unfair today."

It begins with a small adjustment. A little less honesty. A little less empathy. A little more benefit for me, a little less burden for you. The scale tilts quietly before the damage becomes visible.

That is why Proverbs brings holiness down to the counter, the contract, the conversation, the checkout line, the email, the meeting, the moment where nobody may applaud you for doing the right thing.

But Proverbs reminds us that justice begins in the ordinary. In how we measure. How we speak. How we treat the person who cannot repay us. How we handle small opportunities to be fair.

Making the world better is not always dramatic. Sometimes it is refusing to cheat in a small place. Sometimes it is telling the truth when a lie would be convenient. Sometimes it is bringing warmth into a room that has been running on efficiency and suspicion.

The citizen persona matters here. Wisdom is not only private character. It is public atmosphere. A wise person helps the community breathe better.

Maybe today the assignment is not to fix everything.

Maybe it is to make one room more honest.

One conversation more gracious.

One transaction more fair.

One person feel less invisible.

That may not trend. It may not announce itself as world-changing. But goodness has a way of traveling. A just scale in one hand can become trust in another heart.

So yes, make the world a better place.

Start with the room you are in.

## Remember

Justice often begins with the small measure in your own hand.

## Prayer

Lord, make my small dealings honest.

## Today's Challenge

Choose one ordinary interaction today and make it more fair, generous, or human than required.

Date: 2026-06-12

- Alternative title ideas: What The Storm Revealed; Words That Weather Storms; Still Standing After The Washout.
- Places for Fred texture: Fitness discipline, fatherhood, spiritual formation, music practice, ACPE feedback.
- Potential illustrations: A storm revealing foundation; a workout correcting weakness; musical practice exposing sloppy timing.

## Scripture Citation

Proverbs 12:1

## Verse Text

Verse text is not included in the source brief.

## Working Episode Title

Like A Rock

## Hook

Everybody wants to be solid when the storm comes.

But very few people enjoy the correction that makes them solid.

We want the strength without the training, the stability without the feedback, the rock without the pressure that proved what it was.

## Devotional Body

Proverbs 12:1 is about loving discipline and receiving correction. It is not soft language, but it is loving wisdom.

The verse presses on a truth most of us would rather avoid: your relationship with correction reveals your relationship with growth.

Anybody can say they want wisdom. But when wisdom arrives as correction, the room gets quiet. Suddenly we start explaining. Defending. Reframing. Looking for a softer translation of what we just heard.

That is human.

But it is also dangerous.

Because the correction we reject may be the very tool God intended to use to make us steady.

A father learns this from both sides. You give correction and watch it land badly. You receive correction and feel your own pride flare up. You realize discipline is not punishment when love is behind it. Discipline is formation. It is love refusing to let weakness stay hidden until the storm exposes it.

Think about practice. Music practice, workout discipline, clinical training, parenting, marriage. The correction rarely feels poetic in the moment. It feels like repetition. It feels like feedback. It feels like somebody noticing the thing you hoped they would miss.

But later, under pressure, you become grateful for what correction built.

The note is cleaner. The body is stronger. The apology comes faster. The ego does not have to drive every conversation. The foundation holds because somebody loved you enough to tell you the truth before the storm did.

That is why "Like A Rock" cannot just be about being tough. Biblical stability is not stubbornness. Some people call themselves solid when they are really just unteachable.

Wisdom makes a different kind of strength.

It is strong enough to listen.

Strong enough to be corrected.

Strong enough to change before consequences become the teacher.

The storm does not create the foundation. It reveals it. And correction is one of the ways God works on the foundation before the weather changes.

So if someone you trust tells you the truth today, do not rush to protect your image. Pause. Breathe. Ask whether love might be hiding inside the discomfort.

Because the person who loves discipline is not someone who enjoys pain. It is someone who wants wisdom more than ego.

That kind of person becomes steady.

Not loud. Not flashy.

Steady.

Like a rock.

## Remember

Correction feels heavy because it is building something that can stand.

## Prayer

Lord, make me teachable and steady.

## Today's Challenge

Receive one piece of correction today without defending yourself first. Write down what it may be teaching you.

## Scripture Citation

Proverbs 13:2

## Verse Text

Verse text is not included in the source brief.

## Working Episode Title

Courting Disappointment

## Hook

Words can make promises the heart has not prepared to keep.

That is one reason disappointment shows up so often after big talk. Somebody said the right thing. Somebody painted the picture. Somebody created an expectation.

But the fruit never came.

## Devotional Body

Proverbs 13:2 connects speech with consequence. The mouth is not just noise. It produces fruit. What we say feeds something.

That is a serious thought in a culture where words move fast.

We can post before we process. Promise before we count the cost. Flatter before we tell the truth. Speak peace while planting confusion. Say "I got you" when we have not decided whether we are willing to be dependable.

And then disappointment gets courted.

Not because desire is wrong, but because speech created an expectation that character did not support.

That can happen in romance, in leadership, in friendship, in public life, and around the dinner table. We can use words to build a world we are not willing to inhabit. We can describe a future we have not disciplined ourselves to walk toward.

That is when language becomes dangerous. Not because words are bad, but because words are powerful. They can create hope. And when hope is created carelessly, disappointment has somewhere to land.

The wise person learns to respect that power.

This is where the citizen angle matters. Our words shape public trust. They shape family trust too. A home can only absorb so much overpromising before people stop believing the next sentence.

Proverbs is practical about this. It does not treat speech as decoration. It treats speech as seed. Something grows from it. Either nourishment or trouble. Either trust or suspicion. Either peace or a mess somebody else has to clean up.

So the question is not only, "Was it true?"

The question is also, "What did it feed?"

Did my words feed wisdom?

Did they feed resentment?

Did they feed an expectation I have no intention of honoring?

Did they feed a fantasy because I liked being admired in the moment?

Courting disappointment through speech often starts when we enjoy the emotional reward of saying something more than the responsibility of becoming someone trustworthy.

But wisdom slows the mouth down.

It teaches us to speak words that have fruit behind them. Words that do not just sound good, but can be lived. Words that make the people around us safer, not more uncertain.

Today, let your mouth be honest enough to feed trust.

Say less if you need to.

Mean more when you do.

## Remember

Words feed the future before you arrive there.

## Prayer

Lord, make my words trustworthy.

## Today's Challenge

Review one promise, post, or repeated phrase. Ask whether your words are feeding trust or courting disappointment.
